"The climate has always changed"

Short answer: The climate has always changed, but not on a global scale at the same time. Only the last 150 years show a near global-coherence in the rapid global warming, while previous centuries lack spatio-temporal coherence.

Long answer:

Hasn’t the climate always changed? What’s with all the excitement? Temperatures have risen for a few centuries and have then fallen again. Aren’t we just part of a long natural cycle that repeats itself? While it’s true that natural climate change exists, there is a crucial differences to the current climate crisis. The current changes are happening globally (instead of locally) and simultaneously [1]. Mark Maslin, a climatologist at University College London, said about that topic: “Over the last 2000 years the only time the global climate has changed synchronically has been in the last 150 years when over 98% of the surface of the planet has warmed. This paper [1] should finally stop climate change deniers claiming that the recent observed coherent global warming is part of a natural climate cycle. [2] It's also worth pointing out, that scenarios of unmitigated greenhouse gas emissions produce climates like those of the Eocene (Eocene = geological epoch, a long time ago). We are effectively rewinding the climate clock by approximately 50 million years, reversing a multimillion year cooling trend in less than two centuries." [3]

"It's just the sun!"

Short answer: The changes in temperature over the last decades have been too significant and too rapid as to be linked to solar activity or changes in earth's orbit. Also, solar energy received by earth has been decreasing in the last couple of centuries.

Long answer:

In the graph below, you can see the changes in the earth’s global surface temperature plotted to how much energy earth receives from the sun. If the sun would have an impact on global warming, at least a correlation must exist (it obviously doesn’t). In fact, the overall energy received has been decreasing for centuries now. Another thing worth mentioning: If the sun were the cause of global warming, we would observe warming of all layers of the atmosphere. But the stratosphere is actually colling down. Only the surface of the earth is getting hotter. Why is that? Because heat-trapping gases (also known as greenhouse gases) in the lower parts of the atmosphere are responsible for global warming.

"There is no scientific consensus on climate change"

Of course it’s hard to measure the exact consensus in the scientific community, yet the results of different studies all point in one direction: Almost every paper with a position on climate change (97%) supports the overall consensus of human-made global climate change [1]. Furthermore, above 99.9 percent of scientists publishing today also accept that position [2].

"Climate models are unreliable & don’t match observations"

Short answer: The projections of a big majority of past climate change models (14 out of 17) match the measured GSTM (global mean surface temperature) increase (see quotation). In addition to that, all models are being ‘hindcasted’. Meaning: Tested with known data from the past to measure the accuracy of it’s predictions.

Long answer:

A study compared 17 climate change models from 1970 – 2007, which predicted a change in GSTM (global mean surface temperature) to the measured GSTM. Using the temperature over time metric, 10 out of the 17 models had predictions that match real-world data. Four models showed to much warming (N77, ST81, H88 A, and H88 B), and three models less warming than observed (RS71, H81 2a, and H88 C) [1]. After correction mismatches in modeled and actual changes in atmospheric carbon dioxide [2], we see better performance: 14 out of the 17 models produce results consistent with real-world observations. Two models (Mi70 and H88 C) showed a higher implied TCR, one a lower implied TCR (RS71). Click here to find out more about TCR. (image credit: [1])

Most climate change models, as well as other mathematical models used for future predictions, go through a process called hindcasting. During this process, known data from past events are fed into the model. The then produced result is being compared to the known outcome. Only if the model is accurate enough, it’s used to create actual future predictions. This process is also known as backtesting. [3,4]

"Climate Change isn’t human-made"

Only human-made greenhouse gas emissions can explain the current change in the climate. Natural causes alone would have instead led to a cooling of the planet.
